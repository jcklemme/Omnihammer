### Runtherd

A Runtherd is an Ork slave-driver who keeps the Gretchin mob in line through liberal application of violence and intimidation. Unlike the Gretchin he herds, a Runtherd is a full-blooded Ork — taller, tougher, and far meaner.

A Runtherd is equipped with the following wargear:

Slugga
Grabba Stikk
Grot-Prod

The Runtherd may choose which of his melee weapons to use each assault phase.

**Runtherd's Discipline — Crack da Whip!:** While a Runtherd is alive in the unit, when the unit fails a **BREAK TEST**, **CASUALTY TEST**, **PINNING TEST**, or **REGROUP TEST**, the controlling player may immediately remove a number of Gretchin models from the unit equal to the amount by which the test was failed. If sufficient Gretchin are removed in this way, the test counts as passed. If there are not enough Gretchin remaining in the unit to cover the difference, the test fails as normal and the remaining Gretchin may not be sacrificed for a partial effect.

> *For example: A Gretchin unit with a Runtherd has Leadership 7 (using the Runtherd's value). The unit fails a Casualty Test by rolling a 10 — failed by 3. The controlling player removes 3 Gretchin models, and the test counts as passed.*

### Squig Hound

A Squig Hound is a vicious, semi-trained attack beast kept by the Runtherd to help chase down runaways and menace the Gretchin into obedience. Its snapping jaws and foul temper are more persuasive than any amount of shouting.

A model equipped with a Squig Hound gains +1 Attack. The additional attack uses the Squig Hound's profile (S4, AP—) and is resolved separately from the model's other attacks.

**Squig Hound Discipline:** While the Runtherd has a Squig Hound, the **Crack da Whip!** rule is modified — instead of removing Gretchin equal to the amount the test was failed by, the controlling player removes only D3 Gretchin models. If the D3 result is less than the amount the test was failed by, the test still counts as passed.

### Designer's Note: Running Your Grots

> Gretchin serve a fundamentally different purpose from Boyz. Where Boyz are the backbone of your army, Gretchin are cheap, expendable bodies that fill critical support roles:
>
> **Screening and bubblewrap.** A cheap Gretchin mob strung out across your front line absorbs charges that would otherwise hit your Boyz. Their ***Expendable*** rule means when they inevitably die, your real units don't care.
>
> **Objective camping.** Gretchin are Troops and can hold objectives. Park a mob on a backfield objective and forget about them — at their price point, they're the most efficient way to hold ground you don't expect to be contested.
>
> **The Runtherd decision.** Without a Runtherd, Gretchin at Ld5 will break the moment anything serious happens to them. With a Runtherd (Ld7) and the Crack da Whip! mechanic, the mob becomes meaningfully more resilient — but every failed test costs you bodies. The Squig Hound upgrade softens that cost to a flat D3, making the mob stickier but adding points. Ask yourself: do you need the Gretchin to stay, or are they just a speed bump?
>
> **No Mob Rule.** Unlike Boyz, Gretchin do not benefit from ***Mob Rule***. They cannot use their mob size as Leadership, and they do not roll on the Mob Rule Table when they fail morale tests. Without a Runtherd, a failed test means they break and run — full stop. This is intentional. Gretchin are cowards.
>
> **BS3.** Gretchin are actually better shots than Ork Boyz (BS2). They're not wildly spraying bullets — they're carefully aiming because they're terrified of everything and want it dead before it reaches them. Their weapons are just terrible (S2, AP—). Don't expect them to kill anything with armour, but 20+ Grot Blastas can still threaten light infantry through volume of fire.

### Designers Note: Building Your Mob

> Boyz are the core of every Ork army and the most customizable **TROOPS** choice in the codex. The key decisions when building a Boyz mob are:
>
> **Slugga or Shoota?** Slugga+Choppa Boyz get an extra melee attack from dual-wielding a pistol and melee weapon, making them devastating on the charge. Shoota Boyz trade that melee punch for 3 shots per model at 18", giving the mob serious ranged output while still being able to charge afterward (Assault type). Mixed mobs are allowed — a common configuration is mostly Slugga Boyz with a few Shootas in the back rank for covering fire.
>
> **Mob size matters.** The ***Mob Frenzy*** rule rewards keeping the mob at 10+ models — that extra attack across 20+ Boyz is enormous. Combined with Mob Rule (using mob size as Leadership), bigger mobs are more reliable and more dangerous. But bigger mobs are harder to maneuver, more expensive in points, and a juicier target for blast weapons.
>
> **The Nob question.** Adding a Nob gives the unit a Character for wound allocation purposes, access to Power Klaws and Kombi-Weapons, and a higher Leadership value. But the Nob upgrade costs points you could spend on more Boyz. In larger mobs the Nob is almost always worth it; in small 5-man Trukk mobs, the points might be better spent elsewhere.
>
> **Transport choice.** A Trukk carries 12 models — perfect for a small, fast mob. A Battlewagon carries 20 and is much tougher. Match your transport to your mob size and battle plan.

### DESIGNER NOTES: Nobs
>
> **Nobz as a Platform.** The Nobz datasheet is intentionally the most customizable infantry unit in the Ork codex. By folding Meganobz into the Nobz unit as a per-model armour upgrade rather than a separate datasheet, players can build anything from a cheap, fast Choppa mob on Warbikes to a full squad of armoured Meganobz in a Battlewagon — or any combination in between. This flexibility reflects the lore: Nobz are the Ork upper class, and they kit themselves out with whatever they can afford, loot, or beat out of a Mek.
>
> **Weapon Loadout Logic.** Regular Nobz use a three-slot system: up to two melee weapons (start with Choppa, replace and/or add), one pistol, and one non-pistol ranged weapon. The Big Shoota is an exception — it consumes the model's entire loadout, representing a Nob who has dedicated himself entirely to laying down heavy fire. Mega Armour models bypass this system entirely; the suit comes with its own built-in weapons (Twin-Linked Shoota + Power Klaw) and has its own dedicated swap options.
>
> **Mixed Unit Interactions.** A unit containing both regular Nobz and Mega Armour Nobz is a mixed-speed unit. Per core rules, models of different unit types may move at different speeds so long as the unit remains in coherency. Mega Armour models (***Slow and Purposeful***) always move as if in difficult terrain, while regular Nobz move normally. The Mega Armour models benefit from ***Relentless*** (may fire heavy weapons after moving), but this does not extend to the non-Mega Armour models in the unit. Players fielding mixed units should be aware that the unit's overall mobility is constrained by the need to maintain coherency.
>
> **Mobility Exclusivity.** The Warbike and Rokkit Pack upgrades are mutually exclusive with Mega Armour by design — you cannot strap a jet engine to Mega Armour, and Mega Armour cannot ride a Warbike. If any model in the unit has Mega Armour, the entire unit is ineligible for mobility upgrades. This prevents rules interactions where part of a unit is Jump Infantry and part is Slow and Purposeful.
>
> **Boss Nob Equipment.** The Boss Nob's access to Kustom Force Field, Mek's Tools, and Tellyporta represents the privileges of rank — the biggest Nob gets first pick of the Mek's toys. The back-mounted Tellyport Blasta requires both Mega Armour (the suit provides the power source and mounting point) and a Big Mek Warlord (only a Big Mek would build and install such a device). This is a deliberate fluffy gate — it's experimental tech that only appears when the army's top Mek is personally overseeing the boyz.
>
> **WAAAGH! Banner Restriction.** The WAAAGH! Banner may not be carried by a model in Mega Armour. The banner requires two hands to carry and the bearer gives up all ranged weapons to do so — a Mega Armour suit's built-in weapons cannot be simply set aside.
